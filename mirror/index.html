The Brief

Bring a 30-year-old retailer into the digital era — without losing what made it trusted.

Mirror, established in 1994 as a value-focused clothing retailer, required a complete website redesign. The assignment: create a new logo and responsive e-commerce platform to launch online sales for the first time. The project demanded deep market understanding, customer empathy research, and identification of pain points specific to online clothing shopping.

I adopted Stanford D School Design Thinking methodology to deliver a platform where users can browse and filter products by size, color, and style — with a design system and brand guidelines built from research up.

Phase 01

Research

I conducted interviews with 4 participants — ages 30–37, NYC-based busy professionals who shop online frequently. The approach combined qualitative and quantitative methods through conversational, relaxed interview format and contextual inquiry to observe unconscious usage patterns.

Key Research Findings

Users need free shipping and free returns. They need to save time shopping online. Items must be affordable and stylish. They want timeless pieces they don't have to throw out. They want brand membership and loyalty programs.

Core Problem Statement

Online fashion shoppers need to trust the platform before they trust the product. Speed, transparency, and a clean browsing experience aren't nice-to-haves — they're the baseline for credibility.

Phase 02

Define

I conducted open card sorting with participants to understand their mental models for how clothing categories and content should be organized. Participants grouped unlabeled content cards into logical groupings and described each group — surfacing the navigation structure that would increase findability and reduce friction.

A clean, minimal e-commerce experience built around filtering by size, color, and style. Trust signals embedded throughout — transparent pricing, loyalty mechanics, and a checkout flow designed to eliminate the anxiety that kills conversions.

Phase 05

Test

Usability testing with 4 participants using the inVision prototype. Task: find and purchase an item. Zero errors. All participants completed the task with 100% accuracy and described the experience as "modern, clean, fresh."

Outcome

Zero errors, 100% task completion. Participants specifically called out the need for a floating toolbar (25%), easier toolbar access (50%), and a prominent promotional banner (75%) — all actionable inputs for the next iteration.

Reflection

Comprehensive research is the difference between a website and a product.

Mirror reinforced something I believe deeply: the quality of your design is a direct function of the quality of your research. Every layout decision, every content hierarchy choice, every color in the brand palette was traced back to something a real user said or did. That's what makes the result feel inevitable rather than arbitrary.

Future iterations would benefit from additional testing to uncover further pain points, and a dedicated design track for the brand membership and loyalty mechanics that participants consistently flagged as important.

What I Would Explore Next

A loyalty and membership program designed around timeless pieces rather than trend cycles. A floating toolbar and promotional discount visibility addressed in the next design iteration. A mobile app experience that extends the website's clean aesthetic into native patterns.
