The Brief

Expand Spotify's social layer — without breaking what works.

Spotify's mission is clear: to help people listen to whatever music they want, whenever they want, wherever they want — in a completely legal and accessible way. As the group leader in music streaming, they want to stay that way. To do so, they want to improve engagement and retention by expanding their social capabilities.

I was tasked with defining the best way forward and providing a prototype of the feature, integrated seamlessly within the rest of the app.

Phase 01

Research

When Spotify launched in 2006, it was clear the music industry needed help. Today streaming accounts for 75% of how we listen to music. By 2019, Spotify had roughly 198–210 million users worldwide, with 83 million paid subscribers — nearly twice Apple Music's subscriber count.

I started by zooming out: analyzing the market and performing a competitive analysis to get a broad view of key players. Then I zeroed in on a specific historical signal.

Historical Signal

In 2015, Spotify removed its messaging feature due to low engagement. It was hidden in menus, required too many clicks, and placed an unnecessary navigational burden on users. Many weren't even aware it existed — which signals not a lack of need, but a failure of execution. An opportunity to get it right a second time.

Demographic Research

According to Spotify, 72% of listeners are millennials: 26% aged 18–24, 29% aged 25–34, 16% aged 35–44, 11% aged 45–54, 19% aged 55+. I screened participants on the basis that they were regular Spotify users and focused on millennials as the largest demographic.

Research Interviews

I spent around an hour with each participant, uncovering how they listen, share, and discover music. I compiled and synthesized notes into a single uncovered problem.

"I often ended up listening to the same songs rather than discovering any new music." — Research participant

Users have to leave Spotify to share music, disrupting their experience. 75% said Spotify's curated playlists didn't help them find genuinely new music — one user described the algorithm as an "Echo Chamber." Play a few EDM tracks and you'll hear EDM for the rest of your life.

During interviews, 100% of participants said they want to discover music from friends and check out friend-created playlists. The desire is universal. The infrastructure isn't there.

Core Problem Statement

Users are forced to leave Spotify to share music, breaking their listening experience. Copying and pasting a link somewhere else is a workaround — not a solution.

Phase 02

Define

I conducted a second round of interviews with a narrowed scope, focused specifically on how users share music with each other inside (and outside) Spotify. One user commented: "I don't share music on Spotify because it's not clear how it works."

I needed to organize all of this into something actionable. I built an empathy map to paint a broader picture — using post-its on a wall to surface patterns from what users were saying, thinking, feeling, and doing around music sharing.

Key Insight from Empathy Mapping

Copying and sharing a Spotify link by pasting it somewhere else is not really sharing — it's a workaround to a non-existent solution. The experience breaks the moment users leave the app, and discovered songs disappear unless manually saved.

"I don't share music on Spotify because it's not clear how it works." — Research participant

Phase 03

Ideate

The empathy mapping exercise helped organize thoughts and patterns into a user persona — fictional but rooted entirely in research, representing the target needs and aspirations of our user.

I then used HMW (How Might We) statements to reframe insights into actionable questions, enabling rapid brainstorming sessions. I generated around 40–50 HMW statements, then narrowed to the most impactful four.

How might we allow users to send tracks directly to friends without leaving Spotify?
How might we make shared music discoverable and memorable after it's received?
How might we surface friend-curated playlists as a discovery mechanism?
How might we create a notification system that makes receiving music feel meaningful?

Ideation → Storyboarding

The HMW exercise focused the problem space. I then used Crazy 8s — a rapid ideation technique where ideas become progressively clearer with each iteration. Once the concepts took shape, I moved to storyboarding.

Storyboarding helps visualize how ideas fit into a user's daily life. Per Nielsen Norman Group: "Visual representations of UX stories capture attention, provide clarity, and inspire us to take action." The storyboard directly informed the user flow for sharing music with contacts.

The Solution Shape

A native in-app send flow that lets users share tracks and playlists directly to contacts. A new notification system alerts recipients of incoming music, which is automatically saved into a dedicated playlist — "Music from Friends." Songs no longer vanish after a single listen.

Zero app-switching required

Phase 04

Prototype

Hi-fidelity wireframes were created to accommodate the new screens from the user flow. Screenshots of the existing app were taken and additions were made using Spotify's existing design guidelines — ensuring the new feature felt native, not bolted on.

The user flow covers: finding a track → tapping Share → selecting a contact → sending with an optional note → the recipient receiving a notification → the track landing in their "Music from Friends" playlist.

Hi-Fidelity Wireframes — Sharing Flow

All I needed at this point was a working prototype to test with real users. I used InVision to stitch the hi-fi screens together into a clickable prototype matching the user flow. I simultaneously scheduled usability testing participants — so by the time the prototype was ready, testing could begin the next day without delays.

Phase 05

Test

All the work up to this point boiled down to this moment. Users were given the task of finding music sent from a contact, playing it, and then sending a newly discovered track to another friend — entirely within Spotify, without leaving the app.

100%

task completion accuracy across all participants

0

app switches required to complete the full flow

"Instant familiarity — it felt like it had always been there." — Usability test participant

Participants cited ease of use and instant familiarity of the UI elements. The feature felt native because it was built on top of Spotify's existing design language — no learning curve, no cognitive overhead.

Outcome

Participants completed all given tasks with 100% accuracy. The notification and auto-save mechanics addressed both pain points: the sharing friction and the "songs that vanish" problem identified in research.

Reflection

Music is social. The product should be too.

Spotify offers a great service — the record store in your pocket, everywhere, at a reasonable monthly fee. But streaming optimized for efficiency left something behind: the hand-to-hand, human interaction around music. The know-it-all algorithms that curate Discover Weekly are genuinely impressive, but they can't replicate the moment a friend sends you something and says "this made me think of you."

If Spotify manages to properly address how we interact with music socially, it will make sharing easier and more memorable in a meaningful way. The algorithm can tell you what you might like. Only a friend can tell you what you need to hear right now.

What I Would Explore Next

Collaborative listening sessions, friend activity feeds surfaced within playlists, and social proof on track pages — showing who among your friends has saved or shared a track. The infrastructure built here opens the door to a richer social layer without compromising the core listening experience.
