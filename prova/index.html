The Brief

Closing the gap between teacher and student — one practice session at a time.

As a violin teacher, I noticed a recurring pattern: when students practice more, their technique and self-confidence improves dramatically compared to weeks when they don't practice enough. The problem isn't motivation — it's accountability. Teachers have no way of tracking student progress between lessons, as they usually only see their private students once a week and have no visibility into whether assignments are being worked on before the next session.

Prova — Turkish for "practice" — applies Design Thinking methodology across 80 hours of design sprint work to solve this communication gap between music teachers and their students.

The Core Insight

As Lang Lang put it: "When learning an instrument, 90% is practice and 10% is talent." The infrastructure for accountability simply doesn't exist between weekly lessons.

Phase 01

Research

Studies show that daily practice can dramatically increase the level of learning and retaining information — "practice makes permanent." I started with market research and a competitive analysis to identify the existing landscape of music education apps, then zeroed in on what was missing.

I interviewed 8 students and 4 teachers to uncover how they communicate, assign work, and track progress between weekly lessons. Provisional personas were created early as initial archetypes to guide the interview structure before validation.

Competitive Analysis

Existing apps lacked the combination of collaborative features and calendar integration needed to create a real feedback loop. No product bridged the teacher-student gap with built-in progress visibility and social accountability.

Before validating through interviews, I created provisional personas — initial archetypes based on assumptions — to focus research questions and ensure both teacher and student perspectives were explored in parallel.

User Interviews

I spent around an hour with each participant, uncovering how students and teachers experience the gap between lessons. The interviews surfaced a clear pattern: both sides want more continuity, but neither has the tools to maintain it.

Research documentation from 12 student and teacher interviews

Core Problem Statement

Teachers have no way of tracking student progress between lessons. Students lack accountability structures and feedback between sessions. The weekly lesson is too infrequent to catch problems before they compound.

Phase 03

Ideate

The journey maps focused the problem space. I used HMW (How Might We) statements to reframe insights into actionable design questions — generating 40–50 statements before narrowing to the two most impactful.

How might we help teachers rethink how they can help their students keep track of their progress and activate a new sense of accountability to practice their instruments?
How might we facilitate a continuous feedback loop between the teacher and the student between weekly lessons?

Storyboarding

I used Crazy 8s to generate ideas rapidly, then moved to storyboarding to visualize how Prova fits into a student's daily life. The storyboard scenario: a student arrives unprepared → teacher assigns work via app → student receives notification → student tracks and shares recordings → practice motivation increases.

Storyboard — visualizing how Prova fits into the teacher-student weekly workflow

User Flow

The user flow maps the full feedback loop between teacher and student: assigning work, practicing, recording, submitting, and receiving feedback — all within a single app.

User flow — teacher-student feedback loop from assignment to reviewed recording

The Solution Shape

A dual-role app where teachers assign practice tasks and receive recordings, while students track progress, earn level-ups through consistent practice, and share recordings with their teacher. Ten minutes of focused practice, made visible and rewarded.

Zero friction between lessons

Phase 04

Prototype

Paper prototypes were tested first with 4 participants — achieving a 100% task completion rate before investing in high-fidelity screens. Mid-fidelity wireframes established the design patterns and reusable component library.

The UI used Circular Std — the same typeface used by Spotify — chosen because it appeals across age ranges and feels both modern and approachable. The Prova logo uses interwoven sound waves to represent connection through audio.

Style guide — typography (Circular Std), logo, color palette, and design system tokens

High-fidelity screens were built following a minimalist philosophy — taking design elements away until nothing else could be removed. Priority screens: dashboard (home), profile, and stats. The gamification system uses level progression to incentivize consistent practice habits.

High-fidelity screens v1.0 — dashboard, profile, stats, and recording flow

Phase 05

Test

Usability testing was conducted with 4 participants using an interactive inVision prototype. Participants were asked to complete task creation, assignment to a student, practice recording submission, and social sharing.

Outcome

Participants successfully completed all task flows. The gamification layer and recording-sharing features resonated with 50% of participants as the most meaningful differentiators — exactly the accountability mechanics the research had pointed to.

Reflection

Creating environments where students motivate themselves.

The most important design insight from this project was about motivation. You can't directly motivate someone to practice — but you can design environments where motivation emerges naturally. Visibility, rewards, and social accountability are the architecture of self-motivation.

Prova was designed to make the invisible visible: practice time, progress over time, feedback from a teacher who cares. The gamification isn't a trick — it's a mirror that shows students how far they've come.

What I Would Explore Next

Remaining undesigned screens need to be completed and tested. Future iterations: a parent-facing view for younger students, collaborative practice challenges between students, and an AI-powered feedback layer that can identify technique gaps from recorded audio.
