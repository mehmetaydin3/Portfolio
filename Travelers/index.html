01 · The Tension That Made This Urgent

Three GenAI assistants. Three different trust models — shipping at once.

When I joined the engagement, Travelers had three GenAI products in active development. Each team had invented its own answer to the same questions: how do you show confidence? how do you cite a source? how do you disclose that this is AI?

Each assistant had invented its own confidence indicator, citation pattern, and disclosure model. I flagged this in week one — and it reshaped the scope of the engagement.

01 · Fragmentation Audit · Week One

200+ apps. One component library. Nothing above it.

TDS — the Travelers Design System — had strong atomic foundations: components, tokens, a shared library. But there was nothing between TDS and the finished application. No shell standards. No table standards. No shared approach to where things live in a page.

The result: twelve different shells built on top of one TDS. Delivery teams re-solving the same structural problems from scratch. Business unit patterns drifting into rework cycles. And now AI trust risk sitting on top of all of it.

My framing

"I named the gap the 'missing enterprise UX layer' — the space between atomic components (TDS) and application patterns. Naming it is what unlocked the scope."

03 · One Standup. One Conversation. One Product Decision.

What being embedded means in practice.

This is the slide I want people to remember. Not because it's dramatic — because it's representative of how the actual work happens when you're inside the team.

The moment

A Design Lead said no to GenAI in a daily standup. He framed it as a governance threat to TDS.

In a weekly review, that hesitation would have surfaced two weeks later as a blocker. I was in the standup live, so I reframed in the moment — GenAI as a lightweight augmentation of existing workflows, not a replacement layer.

The outcome

We aligned on a pilot that same day — AI-assisted pattern generation inside TDS, not beside it. That pilot became the GenAI zone framework two weeks later.

The hesitation wasn't resistance — it was a half-sentence in a standup. FDPD-style work lives in those moments. The channel was already open. I was already there.

04 · The Standards I Shipped

Standard 01 · Application Shell

Three shell models. One decision rule.

Small, Medium, Large matched to application scale. Six zones: global nav, local nav, page header, primary content, contextual pane, action bar. Every enterprise tool at Travelers gets mapped to one of these three models before design begins.

Zone 1 — Global Nav Persistent. Brand, wayfinding, account. Never displaced by content.
Zone 2 — Local Nav Application-level navigation. Collapses in S model.
Zone 3 — Page Header Title, breadcrumb, primary page actions. Fixed height per model.
Zone 4 — Primary Content The work zone. Maximum real estate in every model.
Zone 5 — Contextual Pane Detail, metadata, AI assist. Present in M and L only.
Zone 6 — Action Bar Commit actions. Docked to the zone that owns the decision.
Figma export · Shell zone diagram — S / M / L models

Shell zone diagram — S / M / L models. Figma export.

Decision

I refused to lock widths or breakpoints. Standards specify behavior, not pixels. The tradeoff: BUs need judgment to extend — which I accepted. A prescriptive width is a constraint that breaks. A behavioral zone is a constraint that holds.

Standard 02 · Data Table

Enterprise work is table work.

Row vs. bulk actions, selection behavior, column behaviors, and a two-mode density model with explicit when-to-use guidance. The standard covers every interaction pattern an adjuster, underwriter, or analyst encounters in daily work.

Row actions

Single-record operations

Inline actions that apply to one row. Never more than three. Overflow into a contextual menu.

Bulk actions

Multi-select operations

Appear only when two or more rows are selected. Action bar surfaces above the table.

Density modes

Compact · Scanning

Compact (8pt row) for high-volume review. Scanning mode for comparison tasks. Both use tabular numerals and status as color + label.

Figma · Table — high density
Figma · Table — scanning mode

Decision

I standardized interaction, not the data model. Locking column widths would have created BU-specific friction across claims, underwriting, and policy systems with completely different data shapes. The tradeoff: less prescriptive — and significantly more adoptable.

04 · The AI Decision at Screen Level

AI inside the workflow step. Not beside it.

The initial direction was a separate assistant drawer — an overlay panel alongside core screens. I pushed back. In discovery I had watched adjusters skip optional tools. The pattern was consistent: if it requires a mode switch, it gets ignored under load.

Rejected — "beside"

AI drawer · optional · skipped

A separate panel the user opens when they want AI help. In practice: beside the workflow means optional. Optional under delivery pressure means invisible.

Shipped — "inside"

AI within the decision screen

AI suggestions render inside the same screen where the work is already happening. Same context, same moment, same decision. Willingness-to-adopt went from cautious to delivery teams pulling the pattern forward.

Standard 03 · GenAI Zone Framework

The framework behind that decision.

A chat component locks the one thing I can't control — the model's output. Zones lock everything around it: where context lives, where output lands, where provenance appears, where controls sit. The content is unstable. The home has to be stable.

Header
Assistant identity, mode, disclosure. Persistent across the interaction.
Context
What the model sees. Record data, selected content, user-provided framing.
Output
Model response. Variable length. Structured by the output type — summary, recommendation, generation.
Provenance
Sources, confidence signal, last updated. Never omitted — this is the trust contract.
Input
User controls: follow-up, regenerate, dismiss, apply. Actions map to the output type.
GenAI inside workflow step · zone diagram
GenAI standalone — broad topics workspace

Left: AI inside the workflow step — Header · Context · Output · Provenance · Input  |  Right: Standalone workspace for broad AI tasks. Figma exports.

Decision

Zones, not a component. A component would force teams to use a specific interaction model the moment the model's behavior changed. Zones give teams judgment inside a defined space. The tradeoff: more interpretation required, and I accepted that to keep the framework model-agnostic.

05 · How It Keeps Working

I shipped governance without a committee.

Governance dies when you make it a committee. The mechanism I built keeps authorship close to the work and gives reusability a path upward without requiring permission to invent. Three layers, one contribution direction: BUs extend downward; validated patterns promote upward.

Layer 03

BU-specific patterns

Owned by individual business units. Claims, underwriting, policy — each extends the enterprise standards for their domain. Can promote patterns upward through the contribution model.

Layer 02

Enterprise Design Standards

This engagement. Shell + Table + GenAI zone framework. The missing layer between TDS atoms and finished applications.

Layer 01

TDS — UI foundation elements

Travelers Design System. Atomic components, tokens, brand primitives. Domain owned by the TDS team.

The contribution model is the operating system for this layer. A BU pattern used across three or more BUs triggers a review for promotion to Layer 02. Promotion is evaluated on breadth of use, not perceived quality. That kept the incentive structure simple and the politics low.

"Make authorship close to the work. Give reusability a path up." — Contribution model principle, Travelers EDS

Two things I'd carry into any enterprise design engagement.

First: live in client standups, not weekly reviews. The moment with the Design Lead isn't an exceptional story — it's the default mode when you're embedded. Half-sentences in standups are where real blockers surface. A weekly review turns them into calendar events.

Second: AI inside the workflow, not beside it. Zone-level structure for context, output, provenance, and controls survives model changes. Screen-level integration survives delivery pressure. Overlays don't survive either.
