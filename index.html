The Approach

I design products that make
complex systems feel
clear and usable.

Most of my work sits at the intersection of AI, healthcare, and behavior change — where the challenge isn't just what the system can do, but whether people understand it, trust it, and actually follow through.

I spend a lot of time on the moments where things quietly break: when a user hesitates, second-guesses, or drops off without saying why.

Too many decisions at once.

Not enough signal about what happens next.

A system that feels just a little unpredictable.

My job is to fix that. Simplify where it helps, keep the nuance where it matters, and make the next step feel obvious without over-explaining it. I care about outcomes — not just shipping features, but seeing people actually use them.

How I Work

I like getting involved early,
when things are still
undefined.

I work closely with product and engineering to shape direction, not just design screens. That means asking a lot of questions, pushing for clarity, and occasionally being the person who says "this still doesn't make sense yet."

I prototype fast, test with real scenarios, and pay attention to where people slow down. Those moments are usually more useful than any survey.

I have a point of view, but I'm not precious about it. If there's a better idea, we go with it.

Background

Before design, I trained as a classical violist. It taught me discipline, taste, and how small details add up over time — all of which still show up in my work.

More recently, I became a dad. It's made me a lot more aware of time, attention, and what actually matters — which, it turns out, are pretty useful constraints when designing products.

Outside of work: skiing, cycling, and cooking. All humbling in their own ways.
